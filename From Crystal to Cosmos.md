### Prologue: Under a Dark Sky

I grew up far from cities, in places where the night sky was uncontaminated.

Born on the prairies, then off-grid in the mountains of British Columbia, finally on a ranch/farm where the nearest small town was miles away. No streetlights, no hum of traffic, no glow on the horizon. Just darkness deep enough that the Milky Way poured across the heavens like a bright river. I’d sit or lie outside for hours, no distractions, and let my imagination travel. The stars weren’t distant points — they were questions, doorways.

When I came of age, I spent nine years in the Yukon. I loved the raw nature — the silence, the wildlife, the vastness — but it pulled me even further from any intellectual community. The night sky there was something else entirely. On the clearest, coldest nights, the Aurora Borealis would come alive — curtains of green and purple and red rippling across the heavens like living light, twisting and dancing from horizon to horizon. It felt close enough to touch, like the sky itself was breathing. Those displays weren’t just beautiful — they were electric, dynamic, a reminder that the cosmos wasn’t static. Something immense and active was happening right above me.

That open sky gave a kid room to wonder in a way few children get today. It saddens me that light pollution has stolen that view from most people now.

I was one of eight — five brothers, two sisters. Third oldest. A big, busy family where everyone had to pull their weight, but there was always room for curiosity.

From the time I could hold a screwdriver, I was the one taking things apart to see what made them tick. Clocks, radios, anything mechanical or electrical — curiosity always won. Not everything went back together perfectly, but the drive to understand the insides never left.

My father fed that fire. He’d been a radio and radar technician in the RCAF, then repaired TVs and radios for a living. Our home was full of vacuum tubes glowing orange, massive capacitors, circuits carrying enough voltage to clear your mind (and teach respect) if a curious finger touched the wrong spot. Electricity wasn’t abstract — it was real currents, fields, resonances you could see, hear, and sometimes feel the hard way.

The isolated life had its price. There was no clear path to formal higher education. I taught myself computers from the ground up — built machines from discrete components, wrote operating systems and applications from scratch. I even started my own early ventures into AI — crude by today’s standards, limited by slow processors and tiny memory. But the real bottleneck was data: whatever the system knew, I had to feed it by hand. It was more of a mirror than a window — reflecting and refining my own thoughts, but never reaching beyond what I could personally supply.

Those skills opened doors to work, but without “papers,” I stayed on the fringes. Real ability mattered less than credentials in structured settings. I kept riding the edges — good work, decent living, but never quite in the rooms where big ideas were funded or published.

Life has many distractions. For over fifty years I was caught in them — jobs, moves, responsibilities, being what I came to call a “tax slave.” The wonder waited.

Then retirement came. Back on a farm with clear nights again. The internet brought access to information that had been so elusive in small libraries. And modern AI arrived in force — this time with vast data, real reach, a true window.

I took it up immediately. The same old curiosity, now with a partner that could extend far beyond one isolated mind.

After fifty years of distraction, I finally had time to get back to where I was meant to be — allowing my mind to live at what nature had intended it to be: not a tax slave, but a servant of the wonders of nature.

The dreams formed as a child under those prairie and Yukon skies — watching the stars and the dancing aurora — finally had room to breathe.

This book is what came out of that.

A lifetime on the fringes, taking things apart, refusing patches that didn’t feel right.

Until the pieces finally started fitting on their own.

And the clock began to sing.
